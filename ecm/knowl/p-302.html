In how many ways can you arrange the \(n+1\) numbers \(0, 1, 2, \ldots, n\) so that they are not in ascending order? The answer is \(\left( n + 1 \right)! - 1\) since \(0, 1, 2, \ldots, n\) is the only arrangement in ascending order. Now lets separate into cases. Let \(a_{0},a_{1},a_{2},\ldots,a_{n}\) represent an arrangement of these \(n+1\) numbers. If \(a_{0} \neq 0\text{,}\) there are \(n\) choices left for \(a_{0}\text{,}\) and then \(n!\) ways to fill out \(a_{1},a_{2},\ldots,a_{n}\) for a total of \(n \cdot n!\text{.}\) Now let \(a_{0} = 0\) but \(a_{1} \neq 1\text{.}\) There are \(n - 1\) choices for \(a_{1}\) and \(\left(n - 1 \right)!\) ways to complete for a total of \(\left(n - 1 \right)\left(n - 1 \right)!\text{.}\) Now continue with \(a_{0} = 1,a_{1} = 1\) but \(a_{2} \neq 2\text{.}\) There are \(\left(n - 2 \right)\left(n - 2 \right)!\) ways, and so on.
