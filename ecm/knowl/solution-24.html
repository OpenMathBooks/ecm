Solution 1.2.12.1.

Given a group of \(2n\) people consisting of \(n\) men and \(n\) women, in how many ways can one choose a group of \(n\) people? The answer to that question is just \(\binom{2n}{n}\text{,}\) the right side of the identity in question. One could also form the group of \(n\) people in the following way: choose 0 men and \(n\) women in \(\binom{n}{0} \binom{n}{n} = \binom{n}{0}^{2}\) ways, or, choose 1 man and \(n - 1\) women in \(\binom{n}{1} \binom{n}{n - 1} = \binom{n}{1}^{2}\) ways, or choose 2 men and \(n - 2\) women in \(\binom{n}{2} \binom{n}{n - 2} = \binom{n}{2}^{2}\) ways and so on. Now add these disjoint cases.

An alternate algebraic proof is less interesting: Extract the coefficient of \(x^{n}\) from both sides of \(\left\lbrack \left( x + 1 \right)^{n} \right\rbrack^{2} = \left(x + 1 \right)^{2n}\)
