The coefficient of \(x^4\) in

\begin{equation*} (a_0+a_1x+a_2x^2+a_3x^3+a_4x^4)(b_0+b_1x+b_2x^2 +b_3x^3+b_4x^4) \end{equation*}

is \(\sum_{i=0}^4 a_ib_{4-i}\text{.}\) If we substitute \(a_3=0\text{,}\) \(a_4=0\text{,}\) and \(b_4 =0\text{,}\) we get the coefficient of \(x^4\) in \((a_0 +a_1x+a_2x^2)(b_0+b_1x+b_2x^2+b_3x^3)\text{.}\) This exemplifies the idea that we can get a uniform formula for the coefficient of \(x^i\) (namely, sum all \(a_jb_{i-j}\) from \(j=0\) to \(i\)) in a product of two polynomials if we are willing to say that the coefficient of a power of \(x\) that does not appear in a polynomial is 0.
