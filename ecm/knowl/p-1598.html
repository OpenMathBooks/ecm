We can prove the contrapositive. Suppose \(G\) has a Hamilton path \((a_1, b_1, a_2, b_2, \ldots, v)\text{.}\) Since \(G\) is bipartite, we know that \(a_i \in A\) and \(b_i \in B\) form a bipartition of the vertices. The last vertex in the path is either in \(A\) or \(B\text{.}\) If \(v \in B\text{,}\) then \(\card{A} = \card{B}\text{.}\) If \(v \in A\text{,}\) then \(\card{A} = \card{B} + 1\text{.}\) But in either case, we see that neither part has at least two more vertices than the other.
